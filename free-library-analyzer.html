started as a little free library geoguessing project. turned into something broader — any photo with books in it. the hypothesis is that what's on the shelf tells you something about where you are and who's there.

it looks at background architecture, plants, sidewalk style, street furniture, the books themselves (languages, genres, spines), and what people in the background are doing. victorian rowhouses read differently than noe valley bungalows. a shelf of japanese manga in a narrow space reads differently than the same shelf in a brooklyn apartment.

the community map grows whenever someone uploads and confirms a location. only confirmed or corrected locations get pinned — AI guesses alone don't make it onto the map.

on training a custom model — and when it becomes worth it
this currently uses a general-purpose vision model zero-shot. it works surprisingly well because it already knows what victorian architecture, bay area succulents, and german street signs look like. but a dedicated model trained specifically on these photos would eventually outperform it — especially for fine-grained tasks like distinguishing cole valley from inner sunset, or reading the book signal.

~200–500 photos — not enough. zero-shot wins. this is where we are.

~500–2,000 — SF neighborhood classifier starts to compete, ~60–70% accuracy. also the threshold where "books as location signal" becomes testable as a hypothesis.

~2,000–10,000 — worth fine-tuning properly. book signal becomes trainable alongside visual.

10,000+ — CLIP variant territory. real neighborhood + global accuracy. enough data to say something meaningful about what shelf composition predicts about place.

for now: zero-shot is the right call. revisit at 2,000 confirmed uploads.
a note on the training data, courtesy of patrick
the model has been exposed to approximately 5 little libraries from china and other hostels. these are, as patrick correctly noted, pretty easy to guess — the signs are in chinese. this means the model may be overconfident on chinese-language photos while being appropriately humble about distinguishing cole valley from inner sunset. working on it.
sf neighborhood specificity
mission district (murals, painted ladies, dense), noe valley (quieter, bungalows, craftsman), cole valley (near the park, quaint), castro (victorian rowhouses), haight-ashbury, potrero hill, glen park, bernal heights, inner/outer sunset, inner/outer richmond, pacific heights, nob hill. if it gets yours wrong, upload more photos.
