this is a side project. you know those wooden book boxes people put outside their houses? i got curious whether you could figure out where one was just from a photo. turns out: kind of yes.

it looks at background architecture, plants, sidewalk style, street furniture, and the library box itself (paint, stickers, construction quality). victorian rowhouses read differently than noe valley bungalows. succulents mean california. that kind of thing.

the community map grows whenever someone uploads a library and confirms the location. eventually the goal is to have a proper sf map with every little free library in the city tagged.

on training a custom model — and when it becomes worth it
this currently uses a general-purpose vision model zero-shot. it works surprisingly well because it already knows what victorian architecture, bay area succulents, and german street signs look like. but a dedicated model trained specifically on little free library photos would eventually outperform it — especially for fine-grained tasks like distinguishing cole valley from inner sunset, or reading the book signal.

here is roughly what different dataset sizes unlock:

~200–500 photos — not enough. a fine-tuned classifier at this scale will probably underperform zero-shot vision on location guessing because it has not seen enough geographic variety. the general model wins here. this is also where the project currently lives, which is why the current approach is correct.

~500–2,000 photos — starting to get interesting, especially if well-labeled by neighborhood. you could train a classifier competitive for SF-specific tasks (mission vs. noe valley) where the general model is weakest. accuracy probably 60–70% on neighborhood-level guesses. this is also the threshold where "books as location signal" becomes testable — do noe valley libraries skew literary fiction? does the mission skew spanish-language? you need the data to find out.

~2,000–10,000 photos — worth fine-tuning properly. neighborhood-level accuracy climbs. you have enough to start training on the book signal alongside the visual one. this is the range where a dedicated model starts to feel justified over the API cost.

10,000+ — now you have something. city-level accuracy globally, neighborhood-level in SF, and enough labeled book data to say something real about the correlation between what is on the shelf and where the library is. worth exploring a small vision-language model (CLIP variant) rather than just a classifier.

for now: zero-shot is the right call. revisit when this hits 2,000 confirmed uploads.
a note on the training data, courtesy of patrick
the model has been exposed to approximately 5 little libraries from china and other hostels. these are, as patrick correctly noted, pretty easy to guess — the signs are in chinese. this means the model may be overconfident on chinese-language photos while being appropriately humble about distinguishing cole valley from inner sunset. working on it.
sf neighborhood specificity
trained (loosely) on the following distinction logic: mission district (murals, painted ladies, dense), noe valley (quieter, bungalows, craftsman), cole valley (near the park, quaint), castro (victorian rowhouses), haight-ashbury, potrero hill, glen park, bernal heights, inner/outer sunset, inner/outer richmond, pacific heights, nob hill. if it gets your neighborhood wrong, upload more photos.
